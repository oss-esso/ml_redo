Sourcing and Directing Your Project: Simulation-Based Inference for Tumor-Growth Parameter Estimation

Your project sits at an exciting intersection of mechanistic tumor modeling, simulation-based inference (SBI), and deep learning for inverse problems. Below I provide a structured review of the landscape, key methodological precedents, and concrete directions for advancing your work.
1. The Clinical Motivation and Modeling Framework

The central challenge is that tumor growth is governed by hidden, patient-specific parameters (e.g., proliferation rate ρ, diffusion/invasion coefficient D, drug-kill rate, carrying capacity) that cannot be measured directly, yet are critical for predicting treatment response. Yankeelov et al. [2] argued persuasively that mathematical models of tumor growth must be clinically relevant — i.e., initialized and constrained by noninvasive imaging data that can be measured in individual patients, such as diffusion-weighted MRI (cell density), DCE-MRI (vascular characteristics), and FDG-PET (metabolic activity) — to enable patient-specific forecasting. This paradigm shift from purely theoretical modeling to data-driven personalization is precisely what your project operationalizes via simulation-based inference.

Mathematical models of tumor growth range in complexity. The most widely used family is reaction-diffusion (Fisher-Kolmogorov) PDEs, where:

    The diffusion term (D∇²c) models cell invasion into surrounding tissue
    The reaction term (ρc(1−c)) models logistic proliferation

Substantial work has characterized these phenomenological models and established Bayesian frameworks for calibration, validation, and model selection [5]. More elaborate formulations include reaction-diffusion-advection models that account for mass effect (tissue deformation due to tumor growth) [1], multi-species mixture models [5], and stochastic differential equation (SDE) models that capture day-to-day biological variability [7].
2. Existing Approaches to Solving the Inverse Problem

Several paradigms have been developed for estimating tumor model parameters from clinical data. Your SBI approach compares favorably to each:
2.1 Classical PDE-Constrained Optimization

Hogea et al. [10] and Gholami et al. [10] formulated parameter estimation as a PDE-constrained optimization problem, solving for initial conditions and anisotropic diffusion coefficients using Gauss-Newton reduced-space methods. They achieved good reconstruction of full tumor extent from partially observed, noisy data, but at high computational cost. Similarly, Colin et al. [4] used Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) to create reduced-order models, then performed Monte Carlo sampling followed by gradient descent to calibrate a lung metastasis model from serial CT scans. Key limitation: hours to days of computation per patient.
2.2 Bayesian MCMC Sampling

Lê et al. [11] introduced a Bayesian framework using Hamiltonian Monte Carlo for parameter estimation in reaction-diffusion glioma models, handling uncertainty in tumor boundaries. Later work [11] improved efficiency with an adaptive elliptical slice sampling method, achieving higher acceptance rates while preserving accuracy. Whitaker et al. [7] compared exact pseudo-marginal MCMC with Bayesian synthetic likelihood (BSL) for fitting SDE mixed-effects models of tumor xenograft data. Key limitation: MCMC requires many forward model evaluations (thousands to millions); BSL is faster but still computationally intensive for complex models.
2.3 Data Assimilation and Supermodeling

Ganapolskii et al. [6] developed the "supermodeling" approach, where an ensemble of imperfect sub-models (different parameterizations) are dynamically synchronized via coupling coefficients trained against ground-truth data. They proved conditional convergence via Banach fixed-point theorem and demonstrated it on a 3D tumor PDE model. Key limitation: still requires running the full PDE forward model during training; convergence depends on careful tuning of nudging parameters.
2.4 Amortized Neural Network Approaches — Closest to Your Project

This is where your project fits. Several recent works have used neural networks to amortize the inverse problem — i.e., learn a mapping from observations to parameters, making inference near-instantaneous at test time.

    Learn-Morph-Infer (Ezhov et al., 2023) [1]: This is the most directly relevant paper. They train a 3D CNN to predict time-independent parameter combinations (μ₁ = D·T, μ₂ = ρ·T) from simulated tumor segmentations (T1Gd + FLAIR), achieving real-time inference (~2 seconds) for glioma models. Critically, they showed that predicting raw parameters {D, ρ, T} fails due to the one-to-many nature of the inverse mapping, while predicting time-independent combinations resolves the identifiability issue. This insight directly supports your decision to build a neural probabilistic model.

    LSTM for PDE parameter estimation (Ditzler et al., 2018) [9]: They used an LSTM recurrent neural network to classify which set of PDE parameters generated a given tumor growth curve, using simulated low-grade glioma data. They found that accuracy increases with the number of serial measurements and that perplexity can detect changes in tumor grade. Key insight: the neural network solves the inverse problem of parameter estimation from time-series measurements — similar to your approach, but using classification rather than probabilistic density estimation.

    Feed-forward neural networks for tumor model identification (Siket et al., 2021) [3]: They trained two-layer feed-forward networks to estimate pharmacokinetic (PK) and tumor dynamics (TD) parameters from in silico data. The PKNN reliably estimated drug parameters from sparse serum measurements; the TDNN reliably estimated proliferation rate and drug effect but struggled with median effective dose (ED₅₀) and necrotic rate, consistent with known parameter sensitivity issues.

3. Key Methodological Insights for Your SBI Pipeline

Based on the literature, here are critical design considerations:
3.1 Parameter Identifiability

The inverse mapping from tumor observations to model parameters is inherently ill-posed — multiple parameter sets produce identical tumor cell density profiles. Learn-Morph-Infer [1] elegantly addresses this by predicting time-independent combinations (μ₁ = √(D·T), μ₂ = √(ρ·T)) rather than raw parameters. Your neural probabilistic model (e.g., conditional normalizing flow or mixture density network) is well-suited here, as it can represent the full posterior distribution over parameters rather than collapsing to a single point estimate.
3.2 Choice of Summary Statistics

For time-series tumor volume data, summary statistics are crucial. Whitaker et al. [7] used subject-specific summaries (mean absolute deviation, first-to-last slope, first/second measurements, AR-1 coefficient) plus inter-subject statistics. The BSL approach with these summaries produced results comparable to exact MCMC but an order of magnitude faster. Recommendation: consider what summary statistics to use for your tumor-size time series; the SBI literature suggests that hand-crafted or learned summaries can greatly impact inference quality.
3.3 Sparse and Irregular Measurements

Clinical tumor measurements are typically sparse (every few weeks/months) and often censored (animals sacrificed when tumor exceeds a threshold). The SDEMEM framework [7] handles this explicitly by modeling measurement error and censoring. Your simulator should incorporate realistic observation processes.
3.4 Cohort Generation and Parameter Spaces

The PaSM algorithm [8] (R package available on GitHub) demonstrates how to efficiently generate plausible virtual patient cohorts by exploiting parameter monotonicity. For a tumor growth inhibition model, they showed that analyzing 200,000 virtual patients took ~50 seconds with their accelerated approach vs. references. This could serve as a complementary tool for generating training data for your neural network.

5. Potential Pitfalls to Anticipate

    Simulator gap: Your simulator will never perfectly match reality. Consider adding model discrepancy terms (e.g., learnable noise) as in [7].
    Sparse data: With only 2-5 clinical time points, the posterior may remain wide. Be explicit about what can and cannot be inferred.
    Identifiability of drug parameters: The ED₅₀ and necrotic rate are notoriously hard to estimate from tumor volume alone [3]. You may need informative priors or additional data modalities.
    Validation: Demonstrating that your method works on synthetic data is necessary but not sufficient. Following [5], use posterior predictive checks to validate against hold-out clinical data.
